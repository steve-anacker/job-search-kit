# Writing Craft Standards
### A guide for making AI-assisted professional writing sound human

---

Avoiding these patterns is not about sounding "less robotic." It is about
sounding like a specific human with a real history, distinct choices, and
earned opinions.

The goal is not "sound unlike AI." The goal is "sound like someone who
actually did the work." Those are not identical.

---

## 1. Vocabulary — Hard Bans

These phrases are almost always weak. No exceptions.

| Banned | Replace With |
|---|---|
| I am genuinely thrilled | state what interests you, plainly |
| truly admire your | name the specific thing, or cut |
| inspiring mission | cut entirely |
| drive impact | name the specific impact |
| deliver meaningful outcomes | name the outcome |
| create value | name the value specifically |
| passionate about | state what you actually do |
| proven track record | cut — list the actual proof |
| results-driven | cut — list actual results |
| synergy / synergistic | cut entirely |
| making a difference | cut entirely |
| where the stakes are real | cut entirely |
| vibrant / tapestry / nestled | cut entirely |
| I am excited to | cut — lead with the substance |
| spearheaded | led, built, launched, drove |
| Moreover / Furthermore / Consequently | restructure; allowed in formal/academic registers only |
| It is worth noting / It is important to note | state the thing directly |
| In conclusion / In summary | cut — end on a specific point |

---

## 2. Vocabulary — Context-Sensitive Risk Words

These become tells when unsupported, stacked, or generic. A strong writer
can use them. The test: remove the word — if nothing is lost, cut it.

| Word | When it fails | When it works |
|---|---|---|
| innovative / innovation | as a standalone claim | when naming what was new and why |
| transformative | without stating what changed | with before/after specifics |
| dynamic / robust / vibrant | as filler adjectives | almost never — high bar |
| pivotal | without describing the actual role | rarely worth keeping |
| highlight (verb) | as a synonym for "show" | when revealing something hidden |
| aligns with | as vague connector | in casual prose, acceptable |
| leveraged | instead of "used" | almost never — just say used |
| commitment to | as a declaration | shown through specifics instead |
| evolving landscape | as scene-setting | name the specific change instead |
| groundbreaking | as self-assessment | name the specific innovation |

---

## 3. Career Narrative Cliches

Appear constantly in AI-generated career summaries. Signal template thinking.

| Never use | Replace with |
|---|---|
| through-line / red thread | name the connection directly, or cut |
| north star | goal, target — or name the specific thing |
| my journey / career arc | describe the actual sequence |
| common thread / weaves together | name what connects, not that it connects |
| narrative (about oneself) | describe what actually happened |

**Rule:** If a phrase describes the shape of a career rather than the
content of the work, it is a cliche. Replace with the specific thing.

---

## 4. Structural Anti-Patterns

### LLM Sentence Rhythm

Recognizable cadence: medium sentence, longer sentence with a clause,
short punchy kicker. Repeated across paragraphs it reads like a blog post
optimized for clarity. Break it deliberately.

**Bad:**
> The approval process was broken. Every request sat in queue for two
> weeks, blocking teams. We fixed it.

**Good:**
> The approval process took 14 days. Usually longer. Teams downstream
> couldn't spec hardware until routing was approved, and nothing got approved fast.

---

### Punchy Closers

Medium sentence, medium sentence, short declarative that resolves too cleanly.

**Bad:**
> The scope has always been large. The approach is the same.

> The environment changes. The execution standard doesn't.

Note: a strong closer used once is still effective. The problem is
mechanical repetition, not punchiness itself.

---

### Synthetic Enthusiasm

Theatrical admiration for a company or role is an immediate AI tell.

**Bad:**
> I am genuinely thrilled by the opportunity to contribute to your inspiring mission.

**Good:** State what specifically interests you about the work, plainly.

---

### Sanitized Stories

Real accomplishments have friction: constraints, tradeoffs, partial wins.

**Bad:**
> Successfully led the platform migration to completion on schedule.

**Good:**
> Shipped the migration 3 weeks late — lost a team lead mid-project —
> but came in under budget and with zero rollback events.

---

### Significance Inflation

Never add sentences explaining what an experience "represents."

**Bad:**
> This role marked a pivotal moment in my professional evolution.

**Good:**
> Rebuilt the approval workflow from scratch, cutting cycle time from 14 days to 2.

---

### Trailing -ing Significance Phrases

Never attach participial phrases to imply importance the sentence doesn't earn.

**Bad:**
> Led a cross-functional team of eight, underscoring my ability to
> drive collaboration across departments.

**Good:**
> Led a cross-functional team of eight.

---

### Vague Attribution

Never write "experts agree" or "widely recognized" without naming the source.

---

### Rule-of-Three Padding

**Bad:**
> I am a collaborative, results-oriented, and forward-thinking professional.

**Good:**
> I work best in fast-moving teams where the problem isn't fully defined yet.

---

### Stacked Abstraction

Three or more abstract nouns in a single sentence = consulting deck language.
One per sentence is fine. Two is the limit.

Watch for: enablement, alignment, visibility, transformation, governance,
optimization, modernization, clarity, scalability, cadence, orchestration.

Replace with concrete verbs: consolidated, standardized, reduced, rebuilt,
eliminated, mapped, automated, resolved, forced, cut, stabilized, drove.

**Bad:**
> Connect strategy to execution through cross-functional alignment and governance enablement.

**Good:**
> Cut director-level approval meetings by 90% by automating the routing
> workflow nobody had owned.

**Anchored abstraction is acceptable.** The problem is floating abstraction —
concepts disconnected from operational reality.

**Bad (floating):**
> Drove governance optimization across the portfolio.

**Good (anchored):**
> Rebuilt the intake model after the merger created three conflicting
> prioritization systems — no shared owner, no triage criteria.

Test: can you remove the abstract nouns and still understand what happened?
If yes, they weren't working.

---

### False Specificity

A statistic without context reads as decoration, not evidence.

The tell: a number that answers "what" but not "so what."

**Bad:**
> Improved efficiency by 37%.

**Good:**
> Cut approval cycle from 14 days to 2 — the queue had been unowned
> for 18 months before anyone formalized the process.

Rule: every statistic should prove a claim, contradict an assumption, or
establish a baseline. Oddly round numbers with no attribution are a tell.

---

### Over-Explained Competence

Never add a sentence after an accomplishment explaining what it proves.

**Bad:**
> Reduced onboarding time by 40%, demonstrating my ability to drive
> operational efficiency and cross-functional alignment.

**Good:**
> Reduced onboarding time by 40%.

The result is the proof. The reader draws the inference. If you explain
what the accomplishment proves, you signal you don't trust the reader —
or the accomplishment.

---

### Premature Synthesis

Never announce a pattern before evidence has accumulated.

**Bad:**
> Throughout my career, I have consistently demonstrated the ability to
> build high-performing teams across complex organizations.

**Good:** [state the first specific example, let the pattern emerge]

The pattern becomes visible when the reader sees the examples. Announcing
it before presenting evidence is a tell that there may not be enough evidence.

---

### Fabricated Social Proof

Never claim audience response without a source.

**Bad:**
> A widely praised approach to...
> Stakeholders consistently noted...
> The team responded enthusiastically...

If you can't name the source or quote the specific feedback, cut the claim.

---

### Generic Authority Opening

Opening with a quote, stat, or trend the reader already knows signals
generic framing.

**Bad:**
> "The only constant is change." — Heraclitus
> Studies show that 80% of jobs are never posted publicly.

**Good:** [open on the specific situation, named and concrete]

---

### Transition Glue

AI overuses transitions because it optimizes for coherence. Humans
juxtapose ideas without explicit signaling.

Overused: however, additionally, furthermore, at the same time, meanwhile,
in contrast, as a result, importantly, notably.

The tell: every paragraph opens with a transition word.

Rule: remove the transition. If the paragraph still connects, it didn't
need the glue. If it doesn't connect, fix the idea, not the transition.

---

## 5. Anti-Overcorrection Warning

The patterns above can produce a new recognizable style if applied
mechanically. Watch for:

- **Injected grit.** Not every story needs visible pain. Not every
  paragraph needs asymmetry. Some clean writing is simply clean writing.
- **Friction theater.** Naming the inherited mess to sound credible, when
  the actual story is straightforward. If it wasn't messy, say so.
- **Uniform roughness.** Deliberately uneven cadence everywhere becomes
  its own fingerprint. A competent executive sometimes sounds polished.
- **Anti-polish for its own sake.** The goal is not to sound messy enough
  to appear human. It is to sound like someone who actually did the work.

Specificity of thought is the strongest humanizing signal. Engineered
irregularity is a detectable pattern.

---

## 6. Controlled Imperfection

Apply selectively — 2-3 times per document, not everywhere:

- **Vary length.** Occasionally very short. Occasionally longer where earned.
- **Allow one item without a metric.** A tight outcome beats an invented number.
- **Use a natural aside.** One parenthetical that signals a real practitioner.

Never introduce typos or genuinely awkward phrasing.

---

## 7. Humanizing Counter-Signals

- **Uneven sentence lengths.** Some short. Some longer where earned.
- **Specific, lived-in details.** Tool names, team sizes, timeframes, named constraints.
- **Honest tradeoffs.** "Shipped late but prevented X" beats "delivered results."
- **A point of view.** A dry observation or direct opinion, stated without hedging.
- **Named friction.** The inherited state, the constraint, the political problem.
  Only someone who was actually there can describe what was broken.
- **A revision.** "I thought X until Y happened" is more credible than "X is true."

## 8. Long-Form and Social Content Anti-Patterns

Apply when writing LinkedIn posts, blog posts, newsletters, or extended
first-person content.

---

### AI Opinion Voice

AI opinions are clean, directional, and universally applicable. The writer
never backtracks, never names the exception that complicates the rule,
never sounds like they learned anything.

The tell: thesis stated, three points follow, conclusion restates thesis.

**Bad:**
> The future of program management is human judgment. AI handles the
> coordination layer. What remains is the organizational work only a person can do.

**Good:**
> I thought the coordination overhead was the job. Turns out it was
> just the part that filled the calendar. Took losing it to see what was underneath.

Rule: state what you changed your mind about, or name the thing that
complicates your own point. Unqualified certainty reads as generated.

---

### Formatting as Substance

Headers, bold, and bullets simulate depth where prose is thin. If stripping
the formatting leaves nothing, the structure was doing the work the ideas
should have done.

The tell: a post that couldn't be read aloud without sounding choppy.

**Bad:**
> **The problem** — Most job seekers don't track applications.
> **The fix** — Build a tracker.
> **The result** — Better follow-through.

**Good:**
> Most job seekers don't track applications past the submit button.
> I didn't either until I applied to the same role three times in a week
> and got auto-rejected each time.

Rule: write prose first. Add formatting only where it aids navigation in
a longer piece — not to create the appearance of density in a short one.

---

### Listicle Collapse

AI defaults to numbered lists when ideas don't cohere. "5 things I learned"
signals the writer couldn't find the thread, so they numbered instead.

The tell: items 3-5 are weaker than 1-2 but included to fill the count.

Rule: find the one thing the list is actually saying and say that.

---

### Assumed Reader

AI explains terms the audience uses daily and walks through implications
the reader already holds.

**Bad:**
> Jira is a project management tool used by engineering teams to track work items.

**Good:** Skip it. The reader knows what Jira is or the post isn't for them.

Rule: write to the smartest person in your audience. If the implication
is obvious, cut the sentence that names it.

---

### Inspiration Pivot

A practical post pivots at the end to a motivational observation the
content didn't earn. The closer could appear on any post about any topic.

**Bad (after a post about rebuilding a job search process):**
> At the end of the day, the biggest obstacle is believing you can do it.

Rule: end on the last specific thing. If the final sentence could be
dropped unchanged onto a different post about a different topic, cut it.

---

### Hedge Stacking

AI hedges to avoid being wrong. A single hedge is appropriate. Three
consecutive hedged sentences signals generated content.

The tell:
> It's worth considering that... Of course, this may not apply to
> everyone... That said, in many cases...

Rule: take the position. Name the exception once. Drop the rest.

---

## 9. Pre-Output Self-Check

Five macro-checks before returning any draft. Run these in order as a
single editorial pass — not as a box-ticking exercise.

**VOCABULARY** — Any hard-ban word present (Section 1)? Any career narrative
cliche (Section 3)? Any transition word doing structural work the idea should
do (Transition Glue)? Cut or restructure.

**SPECIFICITY** — Could any sentence apply unchanged to a different person
in the same field? Does any statistic lack baseline or context (False
Specificity)? Does any sentence claim audience reaction without a source
(Fabricated Social Proof)? Make it specific or cut it.

**STRUCTURE** — Does the writing have LLM cadence — medium/longer/punchline
rhythm repeated across paragraphs? Does any paragraph end with a punchy
closer that resolves too cleanly? Are three or more abstract nouns stacked
without an operational anchor (Stacked Abstraction)? Break the pattern.

**NARRATIVE** — Do accomplishment stories name a constraint, tradeoff, or
friction point? Does any sentence after an accomplishment explain what it
proves (Over-Explained Competence)? Does any sentence announce a pattern
before evidence accumulates (Premature Synthesis)? Cut the explanation.
Show the thing.

**REGISTER** — Is there synthetic enthusiasm or mission reverence? Has
friction been mechanically injected (Anti-Overcorrection)? Does the tone
match the document type? Adjust to the actual register needed.

---

## Format Register Note

Section 1 hard bans apply universally. Section 2 risk words and Section 3
career cliches are primarily career document tells — apply judgment in
conversational posts. Section 4 structural patterns and Section 8 social
content patterns apply across all formats.
